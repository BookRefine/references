Keeping Better Tabs on Suspicious Persons

Bhavani Thuraisingham is the the Louis A. Beecherl, Jr. distinguished professor of computer science and the executive director of the Cyber Security Research Institute at the University of Texas at Dallas.

Updated January 13, 2015, 10:22 AM

The terrorists who have trained with ISIS and Al Qaeda have become increasingly advanced at using information technology, the Internet and social media.

Deep dives into this kind of data, as well as the individual data of suspicious persons including those who return home after fighting abroad can be used to determine the risk of an attack. Using the data we already have about jihadist activity and terrorists, we can build psychological profiles of at-risk individuals. Then we can keep better tabs on those who fit the profile, even if they have not yet committed crimes. This is a way to focus surveillance on those who show greater signs of a threat through their communications and social media presence, money transactions and behavioral patterns.

Using the data we already have about jihadist activity and terrorists, we can build psychological profiles of at-risk individuals.

But how can anyone truly determine whether a suspicious person is likely to commit a crime? Technology alone cannot solve such hard problems and predictive algorithms must be used with caution. We need to be more vigilant about getting the technologists, risk analysts, privacy advocates, policy makers, lawyers and even economists, psychologists, political and social scientists, and educators to create appropriate procedures.

The great challenge here is to identify the new patterns and trends or psychological profiles with high accuracy and low false positives and negatives. But we now have massive amounts of different kinds of information, much of it changing in real-time, to work with something we did not have back in 2001 when data collection and mining first moved from academia into policy.

The parameters of data collection and surveillance have not been set, as a result of the debate about privacy and security that has played out over the past decade. I believe in individual privacy but in order to prevent terrorist attacks like the one in France, it is essential that technologists work with privacy advocates and lawyers to create a system for collecting meaningful data about people who are suspected of being radicalized. We need policies and laws that outline rules for analyzing data about people to extract meaningful, predictive information, because without it, we cannot use technology to keep us safer.
