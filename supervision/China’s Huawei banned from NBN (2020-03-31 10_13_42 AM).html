China’s Huawei banned from NBN

PUBLISHED: 24 Mar 2012 00:01:48 | UPDATED: 27 Jul 2012 13:20:28PUBLISHED: 24 Mar 2012 PRINT EDITION: 24 Mar 2012
Share Links: email
  • inShare
print -font +font
Reprints & permissions

Geoffrey Barker and David Ramli

The federal government has banned Chinese technology giant Huawei from participating in multibillion- dollar tenders to supply equipment to the national broadband network because of concerns over cyber attacks originating in China.

But Huawei is fighting the ban vigorously in public and by using diplomatic channels. It plans to announce its sponsorship of the Canberra Raiders rugby league football team in a bid to lift its profile in the national capital.

Huawei sources have also hinted that the Chinese government will retaliate strongly against Australia if the ban on the company’s tenders is not lifted.

The moves come despite numerous efforts by Huawei to soften its image. The company has been accused of having links to the Chinese government. Its chief executive, Ren Zhengfei, was a member of the People’s Liberation Army and he has never agreed to a media interview. The issue is now emerging as a major test of relations with Australia’s biggest trading partner.

But the government remains unrepentant with Attorney-General Nicola Roxon standing firm on the decision.

“As a strategic and significant government investment, we have a responsibility to do our utmost to protect [the NBN’s] integrity and that of the information carried on it,” a spokeswoman said.

“This is consistent with the government’s practice for ensuring the security and resilience of Australia’s critical infrastructure more broadly.”

It is understood that the deputy secretary of the Attorney-General’s Department, Tony Sheehan, called in Huawei Australia’s chairman, retired Rear Admiral John Lord, late last year to tell him of the bans.

Sheehan reportedly told Lord not to bother tendering for any NBN supply contracts because they would not succeed. Australian officials are also understood to have advised Huawei that Canberra was aware of Chinese cyber attacks.

The ban on Huawei tenders was imposed despite the firm’s high-profile participation in national broadband networks being installed in eight other countries, including the United Kingdom and New Zealand. Its technology is also the basis of Vodaone Australia’s $1 billion network fix and upgrade and it recently won key contracts with Optus. Telstra does not use much of its equipment.

Huawei Australia has made a submission to the Australia in the Asian Century white paper being prepared by former Treasury secretary Ken Henry, stressing its commitment to Australia. It warns that Australia’s relationship with China has to be “an equal partnership – a two-way street”. He added policy settings based on historical bias or outdated perceptions will see Australia miss out on important economic benefits.

But Huawei continues to face difficulties in several markets such as the United States, where intelligence services are concerned about network breaches and espionage. Its director of corporate and public affairs, Jeremy Mitchell, denied the Chinese government had the ability to force it to provide sensitive information about the NBN.

“Huawei’s participation in the NBN is a matter for the government,” he said. “But we have outstanding credentials in this area and believe we would have a positive contribution to the NBN if chosen.”

Ovum research director David Kennedy said all of NBN Co’s equipment came from Europe or North America despite China offering good quality products at lower prices.

“There would be technical specifications they would be given to provide the correct equipment and it would certainly give insights to the structure of the NBN,” Mr Kennedy said.

Security expert and HackLabs director Chris Gatford said such insights could be used “to tell you where the critical devices sit, it would give you insight to the connection points and help you make an attack far more effective”.

The potential sponsorship with the Raiders, who have been searching for a major sponsor since the team’s deal with licenced club The Tradies expired last year, is the second attempt to back a Canberra sporting team for Huawei. In late December, a four-year $4 million deal to sponsor rugby union side the ACT Brumbies fell apart at the last moment reportedly because the company had then been told it would not be allocated any part of the NBN. The Brumbies deal had reportedly been signed off by Huawei’s board and jersey designs incorporating its logo had been approved.

It has since switched its attention to the Raiders.

Alastair MacGibbon, formerly the Australian Federal Police’s top cyber agent and now managing partner of Surete Group, said he supported the ban if made on security grounds.

“The difference between a US-based Cisco or a Chinese-based Huawei is where they are domiciled,” he said. “I doubt either make back doors in their products because they would have too much to lose ... but they would more than likely help their governments if called in to do so.

“You need to ask yourself [between the US and China] which government has been more loyal to us over time and which would support us in a time of Australian need.”
